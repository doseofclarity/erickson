Erikson's Core Concepts

Epigenetic principle: Development unfolds in predetermined stages.

Psychosocial crisis: Each stage presents a conflict between two opposing forces.

Virtue: Successful resolution yields a basic strength.

Age ranges for the first 5 stages (childhood & adolescence)

What is the central theme of Erikson's theory?
Social relationships shape personality; each stage involves a psychosocial crisis that builds ego strength.
1

Trust vs. Mistrust (Birth–18 months)

Virtue: Hope

Infant develops trust when needs are consistently met. Mistrust leads to fear and insecurity.

Nursing: Encourage rooming‑in, responsive caregiving, skin‑to‑skin contact.

Positive outcome example
Baby cries, caregiver feeds → infant learns the world is predictable and safe.
2

Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt (1–3 years)

Virtue: Will

Toddler learns to exercise will, make choices. Overcontrol leads to shame and self‑doubt.

Nursing: Offer limited choices, praise efforts, allow self‑feeding.

"Me do it!" – encourage safe independence.

3. Initiative vs. Guilt (3–6 years)

Virtue: Purpose. Preschoolers initiate activities, ask questions. If criticized → guilt.

Nursing: Encourage imaginative play, answer "why" questions, avoid harsh punishment for curiosity.

4. Industry vs. Inferiority (6–12 years)

Virtue: Competence. Child learns to be productive, gains pride in accomplishments. Failure → feelings of inferiority.

Nursing: Praise school achievements, support skill development (hobbies, crafts), encourage teamwork.

5. Identity vs. Role Confusion (12–18 years)

Virtue: Fidelity. Adolescents explore roles, develop a sense of self. Role confusion leads to identity crisis.

Nursing: Provide privacy, support exploration of values, offer nonjudgmental guidance, discuss future goals.

Nursing Implications Across Stages

  • Trust vs mistrust – consistent caregivers, minimize separation.
  • Autonomy – offer choices (e.g., “apple or orange juice?”).
  • Initiative – encourage role play, answer questions truthfully.
  • Industry – reward accomplishments, provide age‑appropriate responsibilities.
  • Identity – respect confidentiality, support exploration without judgment.
  • Intimacy – teach healthy relationships, communication skills.
  • Generativity – encourage mentoring, parenting classes.
  • Integrity – life review, reminiscence therapy.
